Part One · The Diagnosis

The Three Sicknesses

They do not arrive separately. They compound — each one making the next harder to resist. The same extractive logic runs from the inflamed cell to the captured attention span to the dissolved neighborhood.

01

Metabolic ColonialismThe body under siege

The economic model where industrial food and pharmaceutical systems mine the human body for profit — selling hyper-palatable, nutrient-poor products engineered to override the brain's natural stop signal. The result: a population overfed and malnourished, chronically inflamed.

02

Attentional CaptivityThe mind enclosed

An economy that profits by fragmenting focus and farming the attention it captures. Sustained reflection — the precondition for meaning — becomes the scarcest resource, engineered out of reach by design rather than accident.

03

Existential FragmentationThe community dissolved

The slow dissolution of the bonds — family, neighborhood, shared purpose — that once held a life together. Disconnection becomes its own physiological crisis, and isolation the default condition of the engineered world.

Part Two · The Framework

M·A·H·A

Not another optimization regime. In Sanskrit, Maha means great or supreme. The framework is a sequence — structural integrity, not willpower — for rebuilding the ground beneath a sovereign life, then extending that sovereignty outward.

M

Mindfulness

Reading the signal instead of silencing it. The discipline of attention turned back on your own state, so the body's alarms become information rather than noise to be medicated away.

A

Authenticity

Aligning action with what is actually true for you, rather than the defaults the system installs. The refusal to perform a life optimized for someone else's extraction.

H

Health

Repairing the biological foundation first. An inflamed brain cannot think clearly; a depleted body cannot sustain reflection. Everything tractable downstream begins here.

A

Action

Extending sovereignty outward — from the body to the household, the community, and beyond. Structure made real through what you build, not what you intend.

Inside the book · Self-Assessment

Three Audits

The book is a field manual, not a lecture. Each diagnosis comes with an honest measure — a way to take a clear baseline of where you currently stand before you begin to rebuild.

Assessment 01

The Biological Audit

A clear-eyed baseline of your metabolic function — energy, inflammation, blood-sugar regulation — and where the body needs repair before anything else can hold.

Assessment 02

The Attention Audit

An honest measure of your attentional health: what holds your focus, what fragments it, and how much of your mind is currently spoken for by design.

Assessment 03

The Community Audit

A map of your real connections and the foundations beneath them — the relational ground that determines whether any of the rest can be sustained.

The Model · A Capable Person

The Nurturing Warrior

Not the stoic hero who suppresses everything and burns out. Not the perpetual empath who absorbs everything and breaks down. Something more precise.

The Warrior

Holds the perimeter

The part of you that sets hard limits, refuses to surrender, and keeps moving even when walking alone. The grit to face enemies — whether doubts inside your head or systems outside it.

The Nurturer

Tends the foundation

The part humble enough to tend the cracks in the foundation. It understands that real strength requires patience and care, and acknowledges pain without playing the victim.

A Warrior without a Nurturer is brittle. A Nurturer without a Warrior is defenseless. The integration of both is the only archetype capable of both dismantling the sickness and rebuilding what comes after.

The Author

Mayone Maha Rajan

Mayone Maha Rajan is a writer and researcher working at the intersection of cognitive science, metabolic health, and systems thinking. He holds a degree in cognitive science from the University of California, San Diego. His work is shaped by a single question: why do the crises of modern life arrive together — and what would it take to reverse them?

He works in the open, holding his claims to a falsification-first standard — stating plainly what would prove an argument wrong, and citing as he goes. He writes from Colombo, Sri Lanka. The Maha Principle is his first book.
